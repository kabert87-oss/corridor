What is The Corridor?

The Corridor is a weekly geopolitical tourism intelligence publication focused on Africa. Published every Monday from Nairobi, Kenya, it analyses the intersection of global politics, economics, conflict, climate and diplomacy with African tourism strategy.

The Corridor exists because tourism in Africa does not operate in isolation. It operates within a global system shaped by power politics, exchange rates, air connectivity, regulatory regimes and shifting demand patterns. Every week, global events alter the trajectory of travel flows. The Corridor distils those shifts into concise, executive-level intelligence for the decision-makers shaping Africa's tourism future.

Every issue follows a consistent analytical structure: from the global event, to the structural shift it represents, to the tourism flow implication for Africa, to the strategic move the continent should consider. The analysis is interpretive, not informational. The Corridor does not summarise the news. It tells you what the news means for African tourism.

The Analytical Frameworks

The Corridor operates through four proprietary analytical frameworks that structure every issue and provide a consistent lens for interpreting global events through the specific dynamics of African tourism.

Sovereign Tourism Architecture examines the degree to which African states control the systems, infrastructure and capital flows that govern their own tourism economies, rather than ceding that control to foreign operators, intermediaries and platforms.

Mobility Corridors maps the geographic routes along which tourist flows move across Africa and identifies the infrastructure, policy and geopolitical conditions that open or close those corridors.

The Corridor Index measures the degree to which a destination captures, retains and reinvests visitor spend within its own economy, providing a basis for comparing sovereign positioning across African markets.

The Displacement Dividend analyses the opportunity that arises when conflict, instability or crisis displaces tourist demand away from competitor regions and positions Africa as the beneficiary of that redirected flow.
